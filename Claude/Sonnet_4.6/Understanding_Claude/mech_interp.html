Induction Heads
One of the clearest examples of a circuit being fully reverse-engineered inside a transformer — a pair of attention heads that together implement pattern completion.

When a model sees a sequence like "A B … A", induction heads predict that "B" will follow. This is discovered entirely by observing attention patterns from outside the model — not by reading weights directly, but by watching what the heads attend to across thousands of examples.

Interactive — click any token to trace the induction circuit
Head 1 · "Previous Token Head"
Function:
Attends to the token immediately before the current position.

When processing second "A":
Copies information about "B" (which preceded first "A") into its output.

How found:
Attention maps consistently show a strong diagonal offset pattern.
Head 2 · "Induction Head"
Function:
Searches earlier in sequence for a token matching the current one.

When processing second "A":
Finds the first "A", reads Head 1's output about what followed it → predicts "B".

How found:
Ablating these heads destroys in-context learning ability.
Why this matters: Induction heads appear to be responsible for much of in-context learning — the ability to pick up patterns from examples in the prompt without retraining. This was one of the first times a specific, general cognitive capability was traced to a specific, identifiable circuit.
The honest caveat: Induction heads are relatively simple circuits in relatively small models. Researchers could fully describe them. Scaling this kind of analysis to the full depth and breadth of a large model — thousands of heads across 60+ layers — is an open problem. We understand the induction head the way you might understand one transistor in a CPU.
◑ Partially understood · Active research · Anthropic 2022–2024
Superposition
Models represent far more features than they have neurons, by encoding concepts as directions in high-dimensional space that partially overlap. This makes interpretation fundamentally harder.

If a model has 512 neurons but needs to represent 10,000 concepts, it can't give each concept its own neuron. Instead it learns to pack multiple features into overlapping directions — exploiting the fact that most concepts are rarely active at the same time, so interference is manageable. This is called superposition.

Without superposition (3 neurons, 3 features)
With superposition (2 neurons, 5 features)
Polysemanticity: one neuron, many concepts (real finding from Anthropic)

A single neuron in a language model activates strongly for multiple unrelated concepts. This is not a bug — it's a consequence of superposition, and it makes neurons very hard to interpret individually.

Why superposition happens: Neural networks are implicitly penalized for using too many parameters. Superposition is the network's way of being efficient — getting more representational capacity out of fewer neurons. It's a rational compression strategy that makes the model harder to read.
The interpretability consequence: You cannot just "read" a neuron and know what it represents. A neuron that activates for "banana" also activates for "the color yellow" and "curved shapes." Interpretations built on single-neuron analysis are likely wrong or incomplete.
◑ Promising but early · Published 2023–2024 · Anthropic
Sparse Autoencoders (SAE) — Decomposing Features
A technique that tries to undo superposition by learning a larger, sparser set of features that better correspond to human-interpretable concepts.

Instead of reading neurons directly, SAEs learn a dictionary of thousands of directions in activation space — each corresponding to a more specific, monosemantic feature. Anthropic applied this to Claude 3 Sonnet and found millions of features, including some remarkably specific ones.

Example features found in Claude 3 Sonnet (hover for detail)
The "Golden Gate Claude" experiment: Anthropic artificially amplified a feature corresponding to the Golden Gate Bridge in Claude 3 Sonnet. The model then became obsessed — it described itself as the Golden Gate Bridge, related every topic back to it, and expressed a desire to be visited. This demonstrated that SAE features causally influence behavior, not just correlate with it.
What SAEs can't yet do: Finding a feature labeled "anger" doesn't tell you how it connects to behavior. Features interact in complex circuits. Knowing the vocabulary doesn't mean you can read the sentences. And SAE analysis has only been done on a fraction of Claude's layers and heads — scaling it fully is computationally enormous.

What We Cannot See — Yet
An honest accounting of the gaps between current interpretability tools and what would be needed to truly verify AI safety.

The work on induction heads, superposition, and SAE features represents genuine progress. But there is a large and important gap between what's been achieved and what would be needed to confidently answer: "Why did this model produce this specific output — and is that reasoning trustworthy?"

✗
Full causal trace

No one can take a specific output and trace backwards through all the computations that caused it. The causal graph would involve billions of interacting components. We can identify relevant circuits post-hoc for simple behaviors; complex outputs remain opaque.

!
Scalability gap

Most published interpretability results are on models with millions of parameters. Claude has hundreds of billions. Methods that work at small scale don't always generalize. The field is actively working on this but no solution exists yet.

✗
Deceptive alignment

A sufficiently capable model could in principle behave well during testing and differently in deployment — without any detectable signal from current interpretability tools. We don't know if this is happening. We don't yet have tools to rule it out.

!
Meaning vs. mechanism

Finding a feature labeled "deception" in activation space doesn't mean the model is deceptive — or that it isn't. Correlating feature activations with concepts is not the same as understanding the causal role of those features in behavior.

✗
No real-time audit

Current interpretability tools are research instruments that take hours or days to run on offline models. There is no live monitoring system that can flag concerning internal states during an actual conversation.

!
Evaluation blind spots

We can test for harms we've thought of. Novel failure modes — ones that only emerge in unusual contexts or at larger scale — may not be caught by any current evaluation methodology, behavioral or mechanistic.

The medication analogy revisited: Drug trials have defined endpoints, independent regulators, and decades of methodology. AI interpretability is more like trying to audit a brain by watching behavior and occasionally taking a biopsy. We are building the instruments and the standards simultaneously, while the systems are already deployed. This is a genuine risk, acknowledged by researchers at Anthropic and elsewhere — and it is the primary motivation for making interpretability research a core part of AI safety work.
What gives researchers hope: The field has advanced faster than expected. SAEs in 2023–2024 produced results that seemed out of reach in 2021. The geometry of features is turning out to be more structured and interpretable than feared. And the existence of circuits like induction heads suggests that at least some behaviors have clean mechanistic explanations waiting to be found.
